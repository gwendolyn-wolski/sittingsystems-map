On Seating That Is Not Meant to Be Sat In

Some chairs are present only to be seen. They adopt the form of seating while withholding its function.

These chairs often appear oversized, elevated, or excessively adorned. Ornament replaces accommodation. Their surfaces signal fragility, expense, or authority—conditions that make the act of sitting feel inappropriate, even transgressive. The body hesitates. The chair performs instead.

Placed in thresholds, storefronts, museums, or ceremonial interiors, this type of seating operates symbolically. It marks status, frames entry, or asserts control over who may rest and under what terms. Though physically accessible, it remains socially unavailable.

In these cases, seating becomes a boundary rather than an invitation. The chair does not hold the body; it holds meaning.

Note 01

January, 2026

On Waiting

Some chairs wait without ever being designated for waiting. Displaced from their intended interior setting and placed in exterior or transitional space, they remain oriented toward the body while temporarily suspending use.

A roadside upholstered armchair and a set of dining chairs at the curb both preserve the visual language of accommodation. Their forms still suggest sitting, gathering, and pause, yet their placement interrupts the activities for which they were made. Occupancy remains possible in theory, but deferred in practice.

In these cases, waiting is produced through displacement rather than design. The chair has not been built to regulate a waiting body; instead, it has entered a provisional condition between use, abandonment, collection, and reassignment. Its original social function remains legible, but unresolved.

Here, waiting becomes a condition of the furniture itself. The chair does not simply hold a waiting person; it becomes the thing that waits.
